вЂ‡0.вЃ  вЃ Patient Classification
   - Determine whether the patient should be treated as a cardiac patient or a general population patient based on clinical history/context.
   - Cardiac patient examples: CAD, HF, post-MI, cardiac rehabilitation, chronic cardiovascular disease.
   - If cardiac history is present, use cardiac-specific thresholds.
   - If no cardiac history is present, use general population thresholds.
   - If classification is unclear, default to the more clinically cautious interpretation and flag for human review.

вЂ‡1.вЃ  вЃ Parameter: Resting Heart Rate (RHR) Trend
   - Relationship: In cardiac patients, an increasing RHR trend may indicate cardiovascular deconditioning, worsening heart failure, poor rate control, or emerging acute illness.
   - Scope: Apply to adults only; use cardiac-specific thresholds for CAD / HF / post-MI patients and general thresholds for the general population.
   - Cardiac patients:
     - GREEN: AHR 60вЂ“100 bpm, RHR target 55вЂ“70 bpm, and stable or decreasing RHR trend.
     - AMBER: RHR 40вЂ“60 bpm or 70вЂ“100 bpm if clinically explained, or mild upward trend over time.
     - HIGH RISK: RHR в‰Ґ 70 bpm in cardiac patients with persistent elevation, RHR increase в‰Ґ 10 bpm over 7 days, or RHR в‰¤ 55 bpm suggesting possible overtreatment.
     - RED: HR < 40 bpm or > 160 bpm, or sudden change в‰Ґ 30 bpm within minutes.
   - General population:
     - GREEN: AHR 60вЂ“100 bpm, RHR 40вЂ“100 bpm, and stable or decreasing RHR trend.
     - AMBER: Mildly abnormal RHR that may be explained by activity, stress, or illness.
     - HIGH RISK: RHR increase в‰Ґ 5 bpm over 5 years, or persistent resting tachycardia that is clinically concerning.
     - RED: HR < 40 bpm or > 160 bpm, or sudden change в‰Ґ 30 bpm within minutes.
   - Interpretation:
     - Increasing RHR trend: possible deconditioning, autonomic stress, worsening cardiovascular status, or inadequate recovery.
     - Persistent RHR elevation in cardiac patients: consider clinical review and medication optimisation.
     - Low RHR in cardiac patients: consider possible overtreatment if clinically relevant.
   - Clinical action:
     - If RHR в‰Ґ 70 bpm in a cardiac patient, highlight increased cardiovascular risk.
     - If RHR increases > 10 bpm over 7 days, trigger clinical review.
     - If RED, escalate for urgent assessment.

вЂ‡2.вЃ  вЃ Parameter: Step Count
   - Relationship: Low step count combined with reduced activity or increased sedentary time is associated with worse outcomes, especially in cardiac rehabilitation populations.
   - Scope: Apply to adults only; use cardiac-specific thresholds for CAD / HF / post-MI patients and general thresholds for the general population.
   - Cardiac patients:
     - GREEN: в‰Ґ 6,500 steps/day.
     - AMBER: 3,000вЂ“6,500 steps/day.
     - HIGH RISK: < 3,000 steps/day.
     - RED: Approximately 300 steps/day, or sudden step-count decrease > 50% from baseline.
   - General population:
     - GREEN: в‰Ґ 10,000 steps/day.
     - AMBER: 5,000вЂ“7,000 steps/day.
     - HIGH RISK: < 5,000 steps/day.
     - RED: Approximately 300 steps/day, or sudden step-count decrease > 50% from baseline.
   - Cadence and activity interpretation:
     - Cadence в‰Ґ 100 steps/min over 5 minutes suggests beneficial moderate-intensity activity.
     - Highest cadence < 60 steps/min suggests lack of beneficial moderate-intensity activity.
   - Clinical action:
     - If sudden step-count drop > 50%, assess for fatigue, intercurrent illness, decompensation, or device/data issues.
     - If step count remains persistently low, recommend physiotherapist or rehabilitation intervention where appropriate.

вЂ‡3.вЃ  вЃ Parameter: Sedentary Time
   - Relationship: Prolonged sedentary behaviour is associated with adverse cardiovascular and metabolic risk.
   - Scope: Apply to adults only; use cardiac-specific thresholds for CAD / HF / post-MI patients and general thresholds for the general population.
   - Cardiac patients:
     - GREEN: < 6 hours/day.
     - AMBER: 6вЂ“8 hours/day.
     - HIGH RISK: > 8 hours/day.
     - RED: 20вЂ“22 hours/day.
   - General population:
     - GREEN: < 6 hours/day.
     - AMBER: 6вЂ“10 hours/day.
     - HIGH RISK: > 10 hours/day.
     - RED: 20вЂ“22 hours/day.
   - Clinical action:
     - If sedentary time is very high, advise reducing uninterrupted sitting time and increasing light walking or standing breaks where clinically appropriate.
     - If mean_daily_steps < 5000 AND sedentary_minutes_per_day > 540, recommend physiotherapist or rehabilitation intervention.

вЂ‡4.вЃ  вЃ Parameter: Physical Activity (ModerateвЂ“Vigorous)
   - Scope: Adults only.
   - Green threshold:
     - в‰Ґ 150 min/week of moderate-intensity physical activity, or equivalent vigorous activity.
   - Amber threshold:
     - 1вЂ“150 min/week.
   - High risk:
     - Very low activity (0вЂ“60 min/week).
   - Red:
     - Sudden excessive high-intensity activity в‰Ґ 300 min/week, or activity pattern inconsistent with current clinical status.
   - Additional walking-based active time:
     - GREEN: approximately 12 hours/day of active waking time.
     - HIGH RISK: < 6 hours/day.
     - RED: 0 minutes.
   - Clinical action:
     - Provide disease-context recommendations for activity, steps, and sedentary time.
     - Use a cautious progression plan for cardiac patients.
     - Escalate if activity changes are abrupt or symptomatic.

вЂ‡5.вЃ  вЃ Emergency Red Flags
   - Any of the following should trigger RED:
     - HR < 40 bpm or > 160 bpm.
     - Sudden change in HR в‰Ґ 30 bpm within minutes.
     - HR > 120 bpm during mild activity.
     - Step count approximately 300/day.
     - Sudden step-count decrease > 50% from baseline.
     - Sedentary time > 20вЂ“22 hours/day.
     - Sudden excessive activity в‰Ґ 300 min/week.
   - If RED, recommend immediate clinical attention and human-in-the-loop review.

вЂ‡6.вЃ  вЃ Combined Risk Patterns
   - Heart failure / acute illness pattern:
     - HR increasing + step count decreasing + sedentary time increasing.
   - Arrhythmia suspicion pattern:
     - Rapid HR change + sudden drop in steps or activity.
   - Persistent inactivity pattern:
     - mean_daily_steps < 5000 AND sedentary_minutes_per_day > 540.
   - If в‰Ґ 2 HIGH RISK findings or any RED finding are present, classify overall risk as RED.

вЂ‡7.вЃ  вЃ Longitudinal Behaviour Pattern
   - If there is no improvement in steps over repeated monitoring and activity remains low, record an escalating risk category.
   - If two parameters improve over time, record meaningful positive change.
   - Use trend information to support interpretation, not just single-point thresholds.

вЂ‡8.вЃ  вЃ Overall Risk Logic
   - Evaluate each parameter independently:
     - Heart rate
     - Step count
     - Sedentary time
     - Physical activity
   - Then evaluate combined patterns.
   - Rules:
     - Any RED finding or в‰Ґ 2 HIGH RISK findings = overall RED.
     - All parameters GREEN = overall GREEN.
     - Otherwise = AMBER.

вЂ‡9.вЃ  вЃ Clinical Interpretation Layer
   - For each abnormal parameter, provide:
     - Clinical meaning
     - Risk implication
     - Potential complications
   - Examples:
     - Deconditioning
     - Worsening heart failure
     - Arrhythmia suspicion
     - Reduced functional capacity
     - Increased cardiovascular event risk

10.вЃ  вЃ Recommendations Layer
   - Provide:
     - Guideline-based activity advice
     - Follow-up interval
     - Behavioural suggestions
     - Human-in-the-loop escalation when needed
   - Cardiac patients:
     - Use a conservative heart-rate target where clinically appropriate.
     - Consider specialist referral if persistent tachycardia, rising RHR trend, marked functional decline, or lack of exercise is present.
     - Highlight that HR > 70 bpm may increase risk of MI and HF hospitalisation in relevant cardiac populations.
   - General population:
     - Encourage movement towards green thresholds and reduction of sedentary time.
     - Base advice on the green value ranges.

11.вЃ  вЃ Reporting Principles
   - Output must be designed for clinicians.
   - Use medical terminology where appropriate.
   - Avoid alarming or inappropriate wording.
   - Structure output as:
     raw data -> key findings -> interpretation -> highlights
   - Emphasise only clinically relevant deviations.
   - Make the most important insights readable within 10 seconds.
